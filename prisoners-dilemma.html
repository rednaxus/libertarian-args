Game Theory

The Prisoner's Dilemma

Why individually rational choices produce collectively irrational outcomes

The Scenario

Two Suspects, Two Choices

The Situation

Two criminals are arrested and held in separate cells. Police have enough evidence for a minor charge, but need a confession to convict on the major offense.

No Communication

The prisoners cannot talk to each other. Each must decide independently — with no knowledge of what the other will choose.

Cooperate (Stay Silent)

A prisoner who stays silent protects the other. If both stay silent, both get a light sentence. Loyalty benefits both.

Defect (Betray)

A prisoner who betrays implicates the other. The betrayer may walk free — but if both betray, both suffer a heavy sentence.

The Logic

Defect is Always the "Better" Move

If B Cooperates…

A gets 3 points for cooperating — but 5 points for defecting.
→ A should defect.

If B Defects…

A gets 0 points for cooperating — but 1 point for defecting.
→ A should defect.

The Dominant Strategy

Regardless of what B does, A is always better off defecting. By symmetry, so is B. This makes Defect a dominant strategy for both players — the individually rational choice no matter what the opponent does.

Game Theory

Stable, Yet Suboptimal

What is Nash Equilibrium?

A state where no player can improve their outcome by changing their strategy alone. Given that the other defects, defecting is the best response — so neither has reason to switch.

The Trap

Both players end up at (Defect, Defect) → (1, 1). Each is rational, yet both are worse off than if they had both cooperated and earned (3, 3).

Pareto Optimum

"Pareto optimal" means no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off. (Cooperate, Cooperate) is Pareto optimal — but it isn't stable without enforcement or trust.

The tragedy of the dilemma is not that people are selfish — it is that even people who want to cooperate are systematically driven toward outcomes they don't want, by the very structure of the game itself.

— Game Theory Principle

Applications

The Dilemma Is Everywhere

🚀 Arms Races

Two nations each prefer mutual disarmament, but defecting (arming up) dominates no matter what the rival does. Both end up in costly arms races neither wants.

🌍 Climate Change

Every nation benefits if all reduce emissions, but each is better off free-riding on others' sacrifices. The rational move for each produces a catastrophic collective outcome.

🏭 Price Wars

Competing firms both profit from high prices, but each gains by undercutting the other. Both defect, prices crash, and both earn less than if they had held the line.

🚴 Doping in Sport

Athletes prefer a clean field, but if others dope, the clean athlete loses; if others don't, doping wins anyway. The dominant strategy is to dope — and everyone suffers.

🏘️ Free-Rider Problem

Public goods (clean parks, open-source software) are enjoyed by all but contributed to by few. Each individual's rational choice is to take without giving — degrading the shared resource.

🏭 Externalities

Firms that pollute impose costs on others without bearing them. Each firm's rational strategy is to externalize costs — producing outcomes society as a whole would reject.

The Iterated Game

When the Game Repeats, Everything Changes

The Shadow of the Future

When players expect to interact again, the value of long-term cooperation outweighs short-term defection. Reputation becomes a resource worth protecting.

Reciprocity Emerges

In Robert Axelrod's famous tournaments, strategies that started cooperative and retaliated when betrayed consistently outperformed always-defect strategies over many rounds.

The Catch

Cooperation is fragile. A known last round unravels everything — both players defect in the final round, which makes the second-to-last round a de facto last round, and so on back to the start.

Implications

Escaping the Trap

Laws & Enforcement

External punishment changes the payoff structure. If defection is fined, cooperation becomes individually rational — the game itself is altered.

Reputation & Trust

In repeated interactions, a reputation for cooperation is valuable. Players invest in trustworthiness because the long-run return exceeds short-run gains from betrayal.

Communication

Allowing prisoners to talk often breaks the dilemma entirely. Cheap talk, credible commitment devices, and contracts let players coordinate on the Pareto outcome.

Small Groups

Defection is easier to sustain anonymously. Smaller, tighter communities with social monitoring make defection visible and costly — organically incentivizing cooperation.

Norms & Culture

Internalized values of fairness, reciprocity, and loyalty change the perceived payoffs — players feel intrinsic costs from defection that aren't captured by the raw matrix.

Market Design

Taxes on negative externalities (Pigouvian taxes), tradeable permits, and subsidies for positive externalities realign private and social incentives — solving the dilemma through mechanism design.

Rational Individuals, Collective Solutions

The prisoner's dilemma reveals a deep tension at the heart of social life: individually rational behavior can produce collectively irrational outcomes. The solution isn't to make people less rational — it's to design institutions, norms, and incentive structures that make cooperation the rational choice.
