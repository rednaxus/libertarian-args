## Critiques of Libertarianism & the Libertarian Party
**`contra-libertarianism.html` · 14 slides**

A multi-dimensional critique of libertarian philosophy and the U.S. Libertarian Party, organised across five lenses:

- **Philosophical** — negative liberty's hollow promise; atomistic individualism; the absence of a theory of the common good
- **Economic** — monopolies, information asymmetry, boom-bust cycles, and how wealth concentration undermines democratic participation
- **Externalities** — how negative externalities (pollution) cause overproduction and positive externalities (vaccination) cause underproduction, justifying intervention
- **Self-sovereignty** — arguments against self-ownership as a foundational principle: its lack of deeper justification, the permission it grants to voluntary enslavement, and its failure to account for vulnerable groups and public goods
- **Electoral** — the Libertarian Party's chronically low vote shares, spoiler accusations, and internal schisms

Closes with libertarian counterarguments and a values-dependent conclusion.

## The Prisoner's Dilemma
**`prisoners-dilemma.html` · 11 slides**

An introduction to one of game theory's central results — why individually rational choices produce collectively irrational outcomes:

- **The setup** — two suspects, separate cells, no communication
- **The payoff matrix** — a visual 2×2 grid showing all four outcomes, with Nash equilibrium and Pareto optimal cells labelled
- **Dominant strategy** — why defection wins regardless of the opponent's move
- **Nash equilibrium** — stable but Pareto inferior to mutual cooperation
- **Real-world examples** — arms races, climate change, price wars, doping in sport, free-rider problems, and externalities
- **The iterated game** — how repeated interaction, reputation, and the shadow of the future enable cooperation to emerge
- **Strategies** — Tit-for-Tat, Grim Trigger, Always Defect, Always Cooperate, and why Tit-for-Tat won Axelrod's tournament
- **Implications** — how laws, norms, communication, and mechanism design (including Pigouvian taxes) escape the trap

## Positive & Negative Liberty
**`liberty.html` · 13 slides**

An examination of Isaiah Berlin's two concepts of freedom and the political fault lines they reveal:

- **Isaiah Berlin** — his 1958 Oxford lecture *Two Concepts of Liberty*, the most cited essay in twentieth-century political philosophy
- **Negative liberty** — freedom *from* external interference; the classical liberal tradition (Hobbes, Locke, Mill, Hayek); rights to property, speech, and contract
- **Positive liberty** — freedom *to* act and self-determine; the communitarian and idealist tradition (Rousseau, Hegel, Marx, T.H. Green); rights to education, healthcare, and basic income
- **Side-by-side comparison** — the two concepts across six dimensions: core question, what constrains, role of the state, key thinkers, rights grounded, and chief danger
- **Where they conflict** — taxation, compulsory education, healthcare mandates, minimum wage, zoning, and drug legalisation as live collision points
- **Critiques of negative liberty** — the hollow freedom objection, structural inequality, and the naturalisation of the status quo
- **Critiques of positive liberty** — Berlin's warning that the logic of "true freedom" has historically licensed authoritarian coercion ("forced to be free")
- **The capability approach** — Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum's synthesis: what matters is the actual set of things a person can do and be
